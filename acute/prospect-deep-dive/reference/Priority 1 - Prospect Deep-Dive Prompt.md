# Priority1: Logistics and Freight Solutions Company

## 1. Company Overview

Priority1 is a privately-held, non-asset-based third-party logistics (3PL) provider founded in 1995 in Little Rock, Arkansas. It began as a freight brokerage arm of its parent company (Priority Wire & Cable) and has since grown into a full-service logistics organization. Dan Berardi serves as Chief Executive Officer, leading a team of ~550 representatives across 50+ offices in the U.S. and Canada. Priority1's core services span Less-Than-Truckload (LTL) freight, full Truckload brokerage (dry van, flatbed, temperature-controlled, drayage, etc.), expedited shipping for time-sensitive cargo, and freight management solutions. The company markets itself as a "one-stop-shop" logistics partner, leveraging a network of 47,000+ carrier partners to handle virtually all freight modes.

With over 1.5 million shipments managed annually and more than 80,000 customers served, Priority1 has significant buying power in the freight market. This scale allows it to negotiate competitive shipping rates for small and mid-sized shippers. Priority1's client base is diverse – the company serves shippers across industries such as manufacturing, retail & consumer goods, food & beverage, automotive, energy, and more. Despite its broad reach, Priority1's focus has been on small to medium-sized businesses (SMBs), introducing these shippers to enterprise-grade logistics technology and pricing advantages that they might not access on their own.

Priority1's headquarters remains in Little Rock, AR, and it has expanded its footprint aggressively in recent years. The company opened its 40th office in early 2022 and continues to grow geographically. As of 2024, Priority1's gross revenue has surpassed $1 billion (reaching roughly $1.3B in 2024), placing it among the top 50 logistics providers in North America by size. In its 25+ years of operation, Priority1 has cultivated a reputation as a fast-growing 3PL with a technology-driven approach and a customer-centric culture.

## 2. Unique Value Proposition (UVP)

Priority1 differentiates itself in the freight and logistics sector through a combination of proprietary technology, operational flexibility, and a high-touch service model tailored to SMB shippers. Key elements of its UVP include:

### Proprietary Technology – "Cabo" TMS

Priority1 has invested heavily in logistics technology to simplify shipping for clients. In 2020 it launched Cabo®, a custom Transportation Management System (TMS) that enables customers to quote, book, track, and manage shipments in one platform. Cabo supports multi-modal freight (LTL, truckload, expedited) and features tools like freight bill auditing and automated dispute workflows, all delivered via a cloud-based, mobile-friendly interface. This in-house technology gives Priority1 control over feature development and integrations, allowing for faster updates and tailored solutions compared to 3PLs that rely on off-the-shelf software. The emphasis on tech extends to API/EDI connectivity with carriers and real-time tracking capabilities, which improves shipment visibility and operational efficiency for customers.

### Group Purchasing Power

As a non-asset 3PL, Priority1 leverages its high shipment volume (1.5+ million shipments/year) to secure discounted rates and priority capacity from a broad carrier network. It has formal partnerships with national, regional, and local carriers in LTL and truckload segments, effectively aggregating freight spend of many shippers to negotiate better pricing. This volume-based buying power is a major benefit for small and mid-sized customers: they gain access to lower freight rates and carrier choices typically available only to larger shippers. Priority1's carrier network (over 47,000 vetted carriers) also means more options and flexibility to find capacity, even in tight market conditions.

### Independent Agent Network and Customer Service

A distinctive aspect of Priority1's model is its Independent Agent program, launched in 2008. Rather than relying solely on in-house sales, Priority1 partners with independent freight agents and entrepreneurs who represent its services in local markets. These agents are supported with Priority1's back-office, technology, and carrier contracts, enabling them to focus on customer acquisition and service. This model accelerates growth and spreads Priority1's reach while keeping fixed costs lower. For customers, it often means personalized, local service – agents are highly motivated owner-operators who tend to provide attentive support. Priority1 backs this with robust centralized customer service and operations teams at its headquarters. The company highlights its "Your product, our priority" philosophy, aiming to exceed customer expectations through responsive service and knowledgeable staff. This high-touch approach, combined with tech tools, allows Priority1 to offer a boutique experience despite its growing scale.

### Comprehensive Services & Solutions

Priority1's ability to handle all major freight modes (parcel excluded) under one roof is a value-add for shippers looking to consolidate logistics partners. From LTL consolidation to full truckload, expedited freight, cross-border (Canada/Mexico), project cargo, and even trade show logistics, Priority1 positions itself as a full-service 3PL. Ancillary offerings like freight bill audit, cost analysis, and logistics consulting add to the value proposition. By simplifying transportation management (as their mission states), Priority1 lets clients save time and make data-driven decisions. This one-stop capability is especially useful for SMBs that may lack internal logistics departments – Priority1 functions as an outsourced logistics partner managing everything from carrier selection to delivery tracking and issue resolution.

### Competitive Commission and Pricing Strategies

For its agent partners, Priority1 offers uncapped, competitive commissions paid twice monthly, making it attractive for agents to join and stay. For end customers, Priority1's pricing strategy is to be transparent and cost-competitive by passing through a portion of its bulk freight discounts. Small businesses that ship via Priority1 can often obtain better rates than they would on their own. Additionally, Priority1 emphasizes speed (instant online quotes) and efficiency – its online quoting tools and quick carrier pay can streamline the shipping process for customers who might otherwise spend considerable time obtaining and comparing freight quotes.

### UVP Summary

Overall, Priority1's UVP lies in blending technology and personal service for the SMB freight market. It offers the sophisticated tools and broad carrier access of a large 3PL, while maintaining a customer-focused, "people-powered" approach that builds loyalty. As one industry veteran noted upon joining the company, "there isn't a logistics company out there who has a better reputation from top to bottom than Priority1." This strong reputation is rooted in Priority1's reliability, its innovative TMS platform, and the tangible cost savings and simplicity it delivers to shippers.

## 3. Competitor Analysis

In the competitive 3PL brokerage landscape, Priority1 faces both large global enterprises and specialized players. Below, we profile five top competitors and compare their services, market positioning, and strengths/weaknesses relative to Priority1:

### Worldwide Express (WWEX Group)

**Overview:** Worldwide Express – together with its sister brands GlobalTranz and Unishippers – forms the WWEX Group, one of the largest 3PL providers focused on the SMB segment. Founded in 1994 and headquartered in Dallas, Texas, Worldwide Express started as a small-package (parcel) reseller and expanded into freight brokerage. Through acquisitions (Unishippers in 2017 and GlobalTranz in 2021), WWEX Group's combined revenue reached $4.4 billion in 2025, making it substantially larger than Priority1. The group has ~2,500 employees and over 55 office locations as of 2023.

**Services & Market Positioning:** WWEX Group offers end-to-end shipping solutions across parcel, LTL, truckload, and specialty freight. Uniquely, it blends multiple go-to-market channels: Worldwide Express handles direct sales (previously franchise-based, now corporate-owned) serving SMB and mid-market shippers, GlobalTranz operates one of the industry's largest independent agent networks (500+ agents) catering to mid-sized and enterprise clients, and Unishippers franchises (200+ franchisees) target small businesses in local markets. This multi-brand structure gives WWEX Group a broad reach. They position themselves as SMB experts with enterprise capabilities, offering small businesses the same freight options and technology that big shippers use. In addition to core freight services, WWEX provides parcel express shipping (via major parcel carriers like UPS) – a service Priority1 does not directly provide – as well as freight consolidation, international shipping, and managed transportation solutions. WWEX Group has its proprietary tech platforms such as SpeedShip® TMS for customers and a robust back-end system from the GlobalTranz side. Their scale and carrier relationships enable competitive pricing and capacity for customers (similar to Priority1's group buying approach).

**Strengths vs. Priority1:** WWEX's primary strength is its scale and comprehensive offerings. It can handle virtually all shipping needs (from an envelope via UPS to a full truckload) under one umbrella, which is appealing to SMB customers seeking convenience. WWEX also benefits from a strong partnership with UPS (since 2008) for parcel services, and a well-known franchise network via Unishippers that provides local consulting to small shippers. In terms of technology, WWEX Group has made significant investments (the updated SpeedShip platform, plus GlobalTranz's advanced TMS for agents) – putting it on par with Priority1's tech capabilities. Another strength is sheer market presence: at ~$4B+ revenue, WWEX can negotiate volume discounts even greater than Priority1's, and its brand family (WWEX/Unishippers) has been in the SMB shipping space for decades, yielding strong industry relationships.

**Weaknesses/Differences:** Despite many similarities in target market, there are a few differences relative to Priority1. The WWEX Group is backed by private equity and built via mergers, which means multiple brands and systems integrated together – this could potentially lead to complexity or less unified culture compared to Priority1's single-brand, organic growth approach. Priority1 often touts its singular focus and internal development (e.g. one proprietary TMS, one company culture) as an advantage; WWEX's brands, while coordinated, still operate somewhat distinctly (for example, an Unishippers franchisee might offer a different experience than a GlobalTranz agent or a direct WWEX office). Additionally, Priority1 emphasizes high-touch service through its agents and employees – WWEX also has that model, but Priority1, being smaller, might provide a more personal feel for some clients. Finally, WWEX's inclusion of parcel services gives it a broader service menu than Priority1; however, Priority1 might counter that by partnering with parcel providers or focusing strictly on freight where it excels. Overall, WWEX Group is a formidable competitor, essentially covering the same SMB/mid-market 3PL space as Priority1 but at a larger scale.

### Total Quality Logistics (TQL)

**Overview:** Total Quality Logistics (TQL) is one of the largest freight brokerage firms in North America and a top privately-held 3PL. Headquartered in Cincinnati, Ohio, TQL was founded in 1997 by Ken Oaks. The company has grown explosively; as of 2024 it operates 60+ offices across 26 states with over 9,000 employees. TQL's gross revenue in 2022 was $8.8 billion, and in 2023 around $6.7 billion (making it roughly 5–6 times the size of Priority1 by revenue). It consistently ranks among the top 3PLs in the U.S. (the #1 or #2 freight brokerage by load volume, often just behind C.H. Robinson in the broker segment). TQL is a privately-owned company (founder Ken Oaks) and has a reputation for aggressive growth and sales-driven culture.

**Services & Market Positioning:** TQL is a full-service freight broker offering a wide range of logistics services. Its core strength historically is full truckload (FTL) brokerage, including dry van, refrigerated, flatbed, intermodal, and specialized over-dimensional freight. TQL also provides Less-Than-Truckload shipping (though LTL is a smaller portion of its business mix), as well as intermodal rail, drayage, cross-border shipments to Canada/Mexico, and even warehousing and drop-trailer programs. Essentially, TQL aims to handle any freight movement; it brands itself as having "one point of contact" service, assigning each customer a dedicated account representative to manage all their loads. TQL's market positioning is more broad than Priority1's SMB niche – TQL serves small businesses up through large enterprises. In fact, TQL has won contracts with Fortune 500 companies and is known to participate in large annual freight RFPs. It emphasizes reliability and 24/7 availability, with a slogan of "Whatever it takes" to get the job done. Technology-wise, TQL has developed tools like its TQL Trax shipper portal and a carrier mobile app, but the company's differentiation has traditionally been its human network (a large force of brokers and salespeople). Culturally, TQL is very sales-centric and performance-driven – it frequently appears on "Best Places to Work" lists and has an energetic environment akin to a trading floor.

**Strengths vs. Priority1:** TQL's biggest strength is scale and capacity access. With thousands of loads moving daily, TQL has an enormous carrier network (70,000+ carriers) and can often cover difficult loads or lanes by sheer volume of relationships. Large scale also means more extensive market data – TQL can leverage market intel across North America to guide pricing and capacity decisions, an advantage for shippers with volatile freight needs. For a shipper, partnering with TQL offers the confidence of a proven, financially strong broker (over $6–8B revenue, which implies stability to pay carriers and invest in solutions). In comparison, Priority1 is much smaller in volume; while it too has a broad carrier base, TQL's name recognition and carrier loyalty might be higher in some circles (carriers know TQL will have regular freight). Additionally, TQL's service menu includes things like warehousing and custom supply chain projects (areas where Priority1 is less involved), meaning TQL can be a one-stop-shop for even complex logistics operations. TQL's nationwide office network is also a strength – with 60+ offices, they have local presence and can recruit talent widely (Priority1 has ~50 offices, so in a similar ballpark, but TQL's 9,000-person team far exceeds Priority1's headcount).

**Weaknesses/Differences:** One potential weakness for TQL, relative to Priority1, is customer segment focus. TQL's vast operation tends to focus on high-volume shippers and transactional truckload freight; small shippers with mostly LTL needs might not get the same attention at TQL that they would at a specialized firm like Priority1. Priority1 prides itself on tailored service to SMBs – whereas a small customer might feel like "one of many" in TQL's big book of business, Priority1 (with its agent model) may offer more personalized guidance for those smaller accounts. Another difference is business model: TQL does not use independent agents or franchises; all staff are employees. This centralization means tighter control of service quality from HQ, but it also means TQL lacks the entrepreneurial local agent element that drives Priority1's growth. Culturally, TQL is sometimes noted for high employee turnover in sales (a common trait in brokerage) – Priority1 might pitch its culture as more supportive and long-term. From a technology standpoint, while TQL has solid systems, Priority1's Cabo TMS is a unique selling point that they market aggressively; TQL's tools are perhaps less differentiated (tech hasn't been TQL's primary public selling point as much as service and coverage are). In summary, TQL is a powerful competitor dominating truckload brokerage; it overshadows Priority1 in size, but Priority1 can compete by focusing on LTL and the high-service needs of smaller shippers – areas where TQL is just one option among many.

### C.H. Robinson Worldwide (CHRW)

**Overview:** C.H. Robinson is a global logistics powerhouse often regarded as the largest 3PL freight broker in North America. Founded in 1905, C.H. Robinson has over a century of experience (originating as a produce brokerage in North Dakota). Today it is a publicly traded company (NASDAQ: CHRW) headquartered in Eden Prairie, Minnesota. In 2024, C.H. Robinson reported $17.7 billion in total revenues, with roughly 15,000 employees and 300+ offices worldwide. Robinson's services span the globe – it has operations in North America, Europe, Asia, and Latin America, moving freight across all modes. By scale and breadth, C.H. Robinson belongs to the very top tier of competitors (alongside DHL, XPO/RXO, etc.), significantly larger than Priority1.

**Services & Market Positioning:** C.H. Robinson offers end-to-end logistics and supply chain services. Its core business is freight brokerage for Truckload, Less-than-Truckload, and intermodal freight, similar to Priority1, but C.H. Robinson also has a major division for global freight forwarding (air and ocean international shipping) and customs brokerage. Additionally, Robinson provides warehousing, enterprise logistics (managed TMS), and consulting services for supply chain optimization. A unique facet is its Robinson Fresh division – a legacy of its produce origins – which supplies fresh produce and manages cold chain logistics for grocery/food industries. The company's flagship technology platform, Navisphere®, is a widely used TMS that clients can use for managing shipments and accessing Robinson's capacity network. C.H. Robinson positions itself as "one of the world's largest logistics platforms" and emphasizes its ability to handle any logistics need: whether a local trucking move or a complex international supply chain. Its customer base includes tens of thousands of shippers, ranging from small businesses to large multinationals – Robinson famously counts companies like Walmart and Target among its clients for certain services.

**Strengths vs. Priority1:** The strengths of C.H. Robinson are primarily in its scale, scope, and experience. It has contractual relationships with 66,000+ transportation carriers (trucking companies, railroads, airlines, ocean lines), which dwarfs the carrier network of most brokers. For a shipper, this means Robinson can almost always find capacity and competitive rates, even internationally. Its global forwarding capability is a major differentiator – Priority1, by contrast, is focused on domestic North American freight (though Priority1 has hinted at expanding international offerings, it does not match Robinson's established worldwide network). C.H. Robinson's longevity and public status also contribute to trust and stability; many enterprises are comfortable awarding big freight contracts to CHR because of its financial strength and compliance processes. Technology is another strength: Navisphere is known for its robust functionality, and Robinson's investments in AI/analytics provide sophisticated supply chain visibility tools to customers. Essentially, C.H. Robinson can be a single-source 3PL for global enterprises, providing everything from freight procurement to supply chain consulting, which is beyond Priority1's current scope.

**Weaknesses/Differences:** For all its strengths, C.H. Robinson's differences from Priority1 may create opportunities for the smaller company. Robinson's massive scale means smaller customers may not get highly personalized service – a local SMB might be a very small fish in Robinson's ocean. Priority1, on the other hand, specifically targets those SMBs and promises that every customer is important ("Your product, our priority"). Priority1's independent agent network can offer a personal touch and entrepreneurial energy that a big corporation might lack. Additionally, Robinson, being publicly traded, faces pressure for quarterly results and has undergone leadership changes and cost-cutting measures in recent years; this can sometimes translate to higher margins or service changes that smaller, more nimble brokers (like Priority1) can exploit by being more flexible on pricing or customized service. Another difference is focus: C.H. Robinson's portfolio is broad (including non-brokerage services like produce sourcing and global trade compliance). Priority1 is more focused purely on freight transportation brokerage and related tech, which might allow it to innovate faster in that niche. However, it's worth noting that Robinson is innovating as well (e.g., its Freightquote subsidiary offers instant online LTL/TL quotes targeting the small shipper segment – essentially a digital competitor to Priority1 for very small accounts). In summary, C.H. Robinson is a heavyweight competitor with unparalleled capabilities, but Priority1 can compete by excelling in the SMB market segment where personal service and niche focus sometimes beat mega-scale.

### Coyote Logistics (an RXO company)

**Overview:** Coyote Logistics is a prominent freight brokerage known for its technology-driven approach and "No Excuses" culture of customer service. Founded relatively recently in 2006 (Chicago, IL), Coyote rapidly grew to become a multi-billion dollar broker, reaching about $2.1 billion revenue by 2014. It was acquired by UPS in 2015 as UPS's entry into the freight brokerage space, and subsequently in late 2024 UPS sold Coyote to RXO (a spin-off of XPO Logistics) for approximately $1.025 billion. With that acquisition, Coyote has become part of RXO, now the third-largest truckload broker in North America. Coyote's exact current size is not publicly broken out separate from RXO, but before sale it was estimated around ~$4 billion in annual gross freight volume. The company has around 3,000 employees (estimate) and is headquartered in Chicago (now with RXO's parent in Charlotte, NC).

**Services & Market Positioning:** Coyote's core business is full truckload brokerage, especially in the spot market for van and refrigerated freight. It also offers LTL brokerage, intermodal, cross-border, and some supply chain services, but truckload has always been the primary focus. Under UPS ownership, Coyote began integrating more managed transportation and dedicated fleet solutions, leveraging UPS's customer base, but it remained mainly a digital freight matching broker. Coyote is well known for its proprietary technology platform, CoyoteGO, which provides shippers with quoting and load tendering tools and gives carriers a digital app to accept and manage loads. This early investment in user-friendly tech set Coyote apart in the late 2000s and 2010s, as it helped streamline traditionally phone-based brokerage. Market-wise, Coyote serves a mix of large enterprise shippers (it has handled Fortune 100 clients) and mid-sized companies. For instance, Coyote famously landed a contract managing a large portion of Coca-Cola's transportation a few years after its founding, demonstrating its ability to compete for big accounts. The company promotes a youthful, energetic brand (often sponsoring college football and boasting about its fast-paced culture). Now as part of RXO, Coyote's positioning is under a bigger umbrella, but it continues to operate under the Coyote name as a division focusing on truckload brokerage.

**Strengths vs. Priority1:** Coyote's strengths lie in technology and operational efficiency, particularly in truckload. From the beginning, Coyote invested in automating the matching of loads to trucks; its systems and algorithms enable quick pricing and capacity sourcing, which can mean faster service for shippers. For customers with a lot of full-truckload needs, Coyote brings a strong carrier network and 24/7 coverage (like Priority1, they operate around the clock). Coyote's brand carries a reputation for premium service in crunch situations – their "No Excuses" mantra implies they will find a way to cover a load no matter what. Relative to Priority1, Coyote historically has been stronger in the large shipper segment and irregular route truckload freight. It also now benefits from being under RXO, which gives it access to RXO's massive platform and resources (RXO as a whole had ~$4.55B in revenue 2024, post-Coyote acquisition). This could translate to even more carrier opportunities and combined technology enhancements. Coyote's digital freight matching capabilities are a benchmark that Priority1 might aspire to – while Priority1 has a solid TMS for customers, CoyoteGO is very carrier-facing as well, having tens of thousands of truck drivers and dispatchers actively using the app, which helps liquidity of freight matching.

**Weaknesses/Differences:** Compared to Priority1, Coyote's focus is less on LTL and small shippers. If a customer's main need is LTL freight or a mix of many modes, Coyote might not be as specialized in that as Priority1. Priority1 is an LTL leader (per its own branding), whereas Coyote is a heavyweight in truckload. So in head-to-head competition for an LTL-heavy account, Priority1's experience and carrier contracts in LTL may win out. Moreover, Coyote does not utilize an independent agent model – it has a large centralized operation (mostly in Chicago and a few other offices). Priority1 could leverage its local agent presence to win business where personal relationships matter; Coyote's service, while excellent, is delivered by a central team which might not offer the same local touch in every region. Another difference is corporate agility: after being acquired by UPS and now RXO, Coyote is part of a larger corporate structure which could make changes slower or priorities different (for example, under UPS there were synergies with UPS's shipments; under RXO, integration efforts might temporarily distract from pure customer acquisition). Priority1, being a standalone private company, might be more flexible on tailoring solutions or negotiating custom deals for SMB clients. In summary, Coyote is a top-tier competitor especially for truckload brokerage opportunities, but Priority1 can differentiate by emphasizing modes like LTL, its one-stop-shop approach, and the personalized service of its agent network.

### Echo Global Logistics

**Overview:** Echo Global Logistics is a Chicago-based 3PL that, like Priority1, prides itself on technology-enabled transportation management. Founded in 2005, Echo grew rapidly (with backing in part from tech entrepreneurs Eric Lefkofsky and Brad Keywell) and went public in 2009. In 2021, Echo was taken private by The Jordan Company (a private equity firm) in a $1.3B deal, and continues to operate as a leading mid-size 3PL. Echo's revenue in 2024 was approximately $3.7 billion, placing it slightly above Priority1 in scale but below the likes of TQL and C.H. Robinson. The company has around 2,700 employees and is headquartered in Chicago, IL, with several regional offices. Echo has consistently been recognized in industry awards; for example, it has been voted the #1 3PL by Inbound Logistics magazine's readers for nine years running (2017–2025), indicating a strong reputation among shippers.

**Services & Market Positioning:** Echo offers a broad suite of transportation services very comparable to Priority1's offerings. This includes LTL and Truckload brokerage (covering dry van, refrigerated, flatbed, etc.), as well as intermodal rail, domestic air freight, and even final-mile services for certain clients. Echo also has a significant Managed Transportation division – acting as an outsourced logistics manager for companies, using its technology and staff to run a shipper's transportation function (often via a tailored TMS called EchoAccelerator). In addition, Echo provides warehousing and distribution support through partners when needed, and has expertise in specialized shipments (e.g. temperature-controlled, hazardous materials, and government/military freight). Technology is central to Echo's value prop: their platform Echo Connect is the core that integrates shipper-facing tools (like EchoShip™ for instant quoting and booking, akin to Priority1's instant quote tool) and carrier-facing tools (like EchoDrive™ mobile app for carriers). They also have an internal TMS (EchoAccelerator) for managing complex accounts. Echo positions itself as a tech-driven, "simplify transportation" company – much like Priority1's mission. It targets a wide range of customers: small manufacturers and distributors are in Echo's base, but it also successfully sells to larger mid-market companies and even divisions of large corporations, especially leveraging its managed trans solutions for those who want to outsource logistics.

**Strengths vs. Priority1:** Echo Global Logistics and Priority1 have a fairly similar profile, but Echo has been in the national spotlight longer and has a few key strengths. One is its industry recognition and awards – being repeatedly named a top 3PL by shippers suggests Echo has very high customer satisfaction. This reputation can make it a trusted choice for new customers evaluating brokers. Echo's technology suite is also very mature: the EchoShip/EchoDrive platforms and Echo's TMS have been iteratively developed for over a decade, providing robust functionality (e.g., automated load matching, online tracking updates, self-service portals) that rivals Priority1's Cabo TMS. In terms of scale, Echo's ~$3.7B revenue means it commands significant carrier volume; its network of carrier relationships and historical data is extensive, which can translate to better pricing or capacity for clients (similar to Priority1's leverage, but on roughly 3x the freight spend). Echo also has experience with managed transportation contracts and complex supply chain projects, which might give it an edge for customers looking for more than spot brokerage. Culturally and operationally, Echo has a large team of logistics experts and a centralized model that ensures consistency – their Chicago HQ houses many support teams (pricing analysts, carrier procurement, etc.) that an agent-based model might distribute. For a shipper, this means a depth of resources behind the scenes to solve problems.

**Weaknesses/Differences:** Given the parallels between Echo and Priority1, differences are nuanced. Echo, being larger and having been public, might have had more emphasis on high-volume accounts and operational efficiency, whereas Priority1, slightly smaller, might differentiate with even more personalized attention per account (especially via its agents). Priority1's agent network can be an advantage in relationship-building; Echo's sales approach is more centralized (though they do have regional offices and sales reps, they are employees). In markets where relationships seal deals, Priority1's local agents could win small accounts from Echo. Another point is that Echo is now owned by private equity – which can sometimes lead to a focus on margins and cost management to drive ROI. If that results in any service cutbacks or less flexibility, Priority1 could capitalize by being more flexible on customer-specific needs or pricing. Furthermore, Echo does not have an independent agent program, which means it may not penetrate certain local or niche markets as well as Priority1's agents who aggressively develop those territories. Finally, while both Priority1 and Echo emphasize technology, Priority1's Cabo is proprietary and marketed as built "with logistics professionals at the forefront," possibly allowing custom tweaks for clients; Echo's tech is also proprietary, but one could argue both are comparable – however, a very tech-savvy client might compare platforms and prefer one interface over the other. In summary, Echo is a close competitor with a strong tech and service reputation; Priority1's path to winning against Echo is likely through its agent-driven sales force and positioning as a slightly more boutique provider in contrast to Echo's larger scale operation.

## 4. Market Position of Priority1

Priority1's position in the logistics and freight market is that of a rising mid-sized 3PL with a strong niche in LTL and SMB customers. The company has steadily climbed industry rankings due to rapid growth (over 500% growth in the five years up to 2021 according to its own reports). By surpassing $1 billion in revenue, Priority1 now sits among the Top 50 freight brokers/3PLs in North America. For instance, in Transport Topics' 2024 rankings, Priority1 was listed around the mid-40s by gross revenue, reflecting its emergence into the big leagues of logistics providers. This scale is significant: it indicates shippers and carriers alike recognize Priority1 as a notable player, not a small regional brokerage.

In terms of industry reputation, Priority1 is seen as an innovative and customer-focused firm. Its rapid expansion and tech-forward approach have been covered positively in industry news; the company often highlights awards or certifications (for example, it is a SmartWay partner for sustainability, and showcases employee recognition in press releases). The quote from its incoming CFO in 2020 that "from a reputation standpoint, there isn't a logistics company out there who has a better reputation… than Priority1," while subjective, suggests that within the freight community (carriers, industry professionals), Priority1 is respected for its integrity and culture. The company's mission of simplifying logistics and exceeding expectations through people and technology resonates with many customers that provide testimonials on its behalf. Being privately held, Priority1 has also had the freedom to reinvest profits into growth and technology, rather than focusing on short-term earnings – this has helped it pursue a long-term strategy of building a national network and cutting-edge TMS.

**Client Base:** Priority1's client base skews toward small and mid-sized shippers – companies that might ship anywhere from a few LTL pallets a week up to a few hundred truckloads a year. These clients span many industries, as shown by Priority1's targeted verticals (manufacturing, retail, food & beverage, automotive, etc.). Priority1 has found a sweet spot with customers who are large enough to need freight management help, but not so large that they have in-house logistics departments or direct carrier contracts. Such clients value Priority1's hands-on support and cost savings. That said, as Priority1 grows, it has been moving up-market to pursue larger enterprise accounts as well. The launch of its Enterprise LTL and Freight Management divisions indicates it's courting bigger shippers who might otherwise use competitors like C.H. Robinson or Echo. Priority1's agent model has allowed it to onboard many diverse customers through personal relationships – some agents bring their existing customer following when they join Priority1, boosting the company's book of business across segments from oil & gas equipment to e-commerce retail goods.

**Strategic Focus Areas:**

- **Technology Leadership:** Continuing to enhance the Cabo TMS and related digital services is a priority to stay competitive against larger tech-driven peers. Priority1 likely invests in features like real-time API integrations (as highlighted in its blog on EDI vs API in freight tech) and advanced analytics to give shippers more insights.
- **Geographical Expansion:** Opening new offices (the milestone of 40 offices in 2022, now presumably 50 by 2025) shows Priority1's intent to establish a physical presence in more logistics hubs. Each office often supports agents and regional sales, which helps in capturing local business and recruiting talent.
- **Service Diversification:** While LTL is the backbone, Priority1 has expanded truckload services (hiring experts in flatbed, temp-controlled, drayage, etc.) and built an Expedited shipping department for critical shipments. This broadening of services, including cross-border to Canada/Mexico and project cargo, positions Priority1 to keep more of a client's freight spend under its management, increasing wallet share.
- **International Freight Offering:** Notably, Priority1 signaled plans to venture into international freight forwarding (with the 2020 CFO announcement mentioning an "expansion into an International offering"). This suggests that to compete with larger 3PLs, Priority1 sees value in eventually handling air and ocean shipments for its customers, making it more of an end-to-end provider. If realized, this would further elevate its market position from primarily domestic brokerage to a more global logistics provider.

In the current market (as of late 2025), Priority1 stands out as one of the fastest-growing mid-tier 3PLs, carving out a strong presence in the LTL and SMB space. Its combination of a large carrier network, advanced technology, and agent-based personalized service gives it a competitive edge in that segment. Clients often choose Priority1 for the cost savings and simplicity ("shipping made simple" is a tagline on its site) paired with the sense that they are a valued partner, not just another account. As the industry continues to evolve – with trends like digital freight matching, consolidation among brokers, and economic fluctuations – Priority1's agile size and private backing may allow it to adapt and capture opportunities faster than some large bureaucratic competitors. However, it will also continually be challenged to differentiate itself from both larger rivals and numerous other mid-sized brokers. Maintaining its tech leadership and customer-first culture will be key to solidifying Priority1's position as a go-to 3PL for years to come.
